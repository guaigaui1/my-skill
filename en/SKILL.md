# Fable Learning

Teach a concept, design idea, or piece of knowledge **indirectly through a fable**. Let the reader only dimly sense what it is near the very end, then add a short reveal afterward. The goal is for the reader to live through the mechanism's cause-and-effect inside the story and go "aha" on their own — before it is ever named.

## When to use

- The user wants to grasp the core of an abstract concept, design idea, or piece of knowledge
- The user wants to distinguish several easily-confused related concepts
- The user says "use a fable / tell me a story / give me an analogy"

## How to write it

**Find the kernel first.** Before writing, get clear on the one thing you're actually conveying — "that single beat." It's the mechanism or the tension, not the definition. One story carries one kernel; cram in more and it falls apart.

**The fable body.** Use concrete people and events, and let the mechanism unfold naturally as cause-and-effect. No jargon. Make the conflict and the turn *be* the concept's causality at work. End on a moment that makes the reader go "aha" — yet without naming it. Write in plain paragraphs; no italics, no blockquotes (`>`); bold key words sparingly.

**The reveal.** Start a new section, separated by `---`. Say plainly "that thing just now is actually X," map the story's key elements back to the concept one by one, and point out why it's designed this way and where it does and doesn't apply. Keep it tight; don't pile on jargon.

Default to English unless the user asks for another language.
